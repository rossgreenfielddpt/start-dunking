Every YouTube Jump Program Told Me to Train Harder. I Did, For 6 Months. Here's What Actually Happened.

I followed every workout. Every drill. Every program I could find. I trained harder than anyone on my team. Six months later, my vert was lower than when I started.

When I sat down at the end of last season and realized I still couldn't dunk, I wasn't sad. I was angry. Because I'd been working on it for almost a year and a half. My standing vert at the end of June was 22 inches. Three inches less than the previous September.

I had trained for nine months and gotten worse.

This is the story of what happened to me, and what finally fixed it. I'm writing it down because I've talked to a lot of guys at my school and at AAU tournaments who are stuck in the exact same place I was. I want them to read this and figure it out faster than I did.

Day 1: The Honeymoon

I started on a Monday, because that's what you do. I'd just bought a new program — I won't say which one, but you've seen it. Big YouTube guy, lots of testimonials, promised 10+ inches in 12 weeks. I downloaded the PDF, set up an alarm for 5:45 AM, and bought a pair of those weighted training shoes I'd been saving up for.

The first workout was intense. Plyometric circuit, depth jumps, calf raises, weighted squat jumps, repeat for 45 minutes. I was drenched. My legs were shaking. I felt like I was finally doing something real.

That first week, my vert went up an inch. I went from 22 to 23. I called my buddy. I told my mom. I bought a new pair of jump-tracking sleeves as a reward.

Week Two: The Plateau

Week two, nothing moved.

I went back through my workout log. I hadn't skipped anything. I'd hit every rep. I tightened up — I added an extra plyo session at lunch. I cut out the rest day on Sunday. I started doing 200 calf raises every night before bed.

Still nothing.

I told myself this was normal. Plateaus happen. My body was adjusting. I just needed to push harder.

What "Train Harder" Actually Does To A High Schooler's Vert
  • High-volume plyometrics done daily without recovery accumulate joint stress faster than they build power — most teenagers' tendons can't handle the load they're being told to do
  • Doing 200+ calf raises a night strengthens an endurance pattern, not the explosive twitch fibers that make you jump
  • Stacking jump sessions on top of basketball practice means every rep is a fatigued rep — fatigued reps train the body to be slower off the floor, not faster
  • The "more volume = more progress" assumption is built for adults with full recovery — applied to a 16-year-old also playing two hours a day, it backfires
  • Most high schoolers doing free YouTube programs lose vert over a long enough timeline. They're not failing the program. The program is wrong for them.

Weeks Three Through Six: The Spiral

By week three I was doing two-a-days. Jump training in the morning, conditioning at night, basketball practice in between. I'd added a depth jump variation I'd seen on TikTok where you drop off a 36-inch box. My knees started hurting. I told myself it was a good kind of sore.

I was tired all the time. Not the good kind of tired that comes from a hard practice — the hollow kind. I'd wake up at 5:30 and feel like I hadn't slept. I started getting beat in 1-on-1 by guys I used to torch. My handle got worse. I felt slower on every drive.

I went home from a tournament in late July where I'd played some of the worst basketball of my life and finally tested my vert again on the gym wall.

21.5 inches.

I'd lost half an inch in three weeks of the hardest training I'd ever done. I sat on the gym floor for a long time.

"I was doing more than anyone on my team. My vert was getting worse. My handle was getting worse. My body felt 30. And every program I'd bought told me the answer was to keep pushing."

Week Seven: Something Is Wrong

By the end of July I had been on this kind of program — the YouTube/TikTok grind-it-out style — for over six months across three different programs. I'd lost vert. I was beat up. I was slower in every direction.

I went to the trainer my school uses for football and told him what was happening. He looked at my workout log, asked a few questions, and said, "It looks like you're working hard. Just stay consistent."

Stay consistent.

I was 16 years old, doing two-a-days six days a week, and a guy who is supposed to know things about training was telling me to be more patient.

I went home and finally Googled something I'd been afraid to Google: "Why is my vert going down."

What I Didn't Know (And No YouTube Program Was Going To Tell Me)

Your nervous system only adapts to high-quality reps. When you're tired, your reps stop being high-quality — they become noise. They train you to be average, not explosive.

Most jump programs ignore this completely. They prescribe huge volumes of mediocre reps because volume looks impressive on a workout sheet. Volume doesn't make you jump higher. Quality reps in a fresh state make you jump higher. You can't have one without the other.

The cruel part: the harder you push when you're already cooked, the further behind you fall. Your body interprets nonstop fatigue as a survival situation and stops adapting altogether. You end up with knees that hurt, a slower first step, and a vert that's gone the wrong direction. This isn't a character flaw. It's biology. And it's what happens to most kids who follow generic jump programs without anyone telling them this.

Why This Happens (The Short Version)

Here's what I figured out. Most jump programs you find online are written for content, not for results. They look impressive on YouTube because they're loud and sweaty.
